Area of interest
  • Sexual Violence
  • Rape Culture, Victim blaming culture
  • Gender Inequality
  • Mental Health
Why this, why now? Sexual violence is an issue that has always been in the society but neglected to be talked about openly in the public because of the sensitivity and complexity of its nature. Sexual violence is not only a problem of people physically and mentally abusing others but a problem that is rooted deeply in the culture and history of inequality, violence, and dehumanization that puts individuals with such experience in high risks in various aspects of their lives from remaining mental and physical health to fulfilling basic everyday needs. 2017 was an important year for this issue. By the help of the power of media, protests, and campaigns, more conversations were opened up to the public, getting people ready to listen to survivors' experience. However, there still seem to be lack of support system for survivors of sexual violence that is understanding of individual's experiences and situations that is sustainable and reliable.

Why won't individuals with experience of sexual violence report?

Interviews
Interview Questions:
General service
  • What is your role in the organization?
  • Can you walk me through the process after a survivor reaches out to you?
  • How do you get survivors to contact the organization for help?
  • What kind of resources do you provide?
  • What are the resources that are most helpful to survivors?
  • When the resources are provided, is there anybody following up with the survivors if they’re getting supported?
Survivor’s immediate environment
  • What is the typical process survivors go through before they reach out to your organization?
  • Do they talk to their friends/family before they reach out to you?
  • What do you think about the impact survivors’ environment have on them?
  • What are the ways survivors can get help from their immediate environment? What can be the negative impacts?
  • What do you think it’s the most important for survivors’ friends/family to know/have to support them better? (emotional understanding/ knowledge to resources/ time/ communication skills, etc)
Reporting
  • Do survivors who contact the organization desire to report the crime? What are the reasons to report and not to report?
  • When survivors do not wish to report, what can be done about the perpetrator?
  • What does the process look like after a survivor request for help in reporting process?
  • Can you walk me through the process?
  • From my research I found that it is sometimes difficult to communicate with survivors. Due to their trauma, sometimes it’s hard for them to remember the attack accurately. How can you/ survivors’ friends/family help in such case?
  • Are friends/family allowed to be there when the survivors try to report?
  • What can be done when the survivor wish to report 48 hours after the attack?
Working with BPD
  • Does your organization engage with the BPD?
  • Can you tell me how and in what cases?
Working with vulnerable population
  • From my research I found that it is more difficult to build a case when the survivor is LGBTQ, or sex worker. Do you provide special service for the minority population?
  • Do you provide any special services for children under 18?
  • Do you provide any special services for male victims?
  • In your opinion, who is the most marginalized population?
Wrapping up
  • Do you have anything else you want to share?
  • Do you have a question for me?
Interview Takeaways
Prevention Education Specialist (former)
YWCA
"The focus was a Victim-Centered; providing options for the individuals"
Interview takeaways
  1. Helping individuals to naviagate their options better and support them to make informed decisions is very important.
  2. Being anonymous can make survivors feel safer because they can disclose their information as much as they can.
  3. Part of the reasons survivors reach out to the support organizations rather than their friends and family is because the they might not know how to response to the situation - Talking to a person who is trained can be more helpful to get the right resources.
  4. People around survivors saying the right things and asking right things can be so powerful for survivors' healing process
  5. Survivors might not want to report not only because of the shame, guilt and complicated emotions but also because of limitations of time, language and other responsibilities.
Abuse response and prevention coordinator
Mennonite Central Committee
"We need to meet people where they're at and go with the pace they're at."
"Everyone has someone in their life who experienced abuse"
Interview takeaways:
  1. It is important to create the space where people can figure out their own needs and decide their own next steps.
  2. Should be mindful not to take power and control away from survivors. This happens often even with good intentions. People who try to help- shouldn't assume what survivors need
  3. Supporting someone can be difficult and emotionally exhausting. One should know how to take of oneself to support others.
Community Educator
Turnaround
"Everybody's experience is different. There is no typical case"
Interview takeaways:
  1. There is no exact steps in providing resources because it should be left up to survivors.
  2. Educations should be delivered considering the audiences' eye level.
  3. A lot of clients of Turnaround are not open to talk about their experience with their friends and family.
  4. The stage where you can talk about your experience with the close people can be very far along in the healing journey.
  5. Not everybody identifies themselves as "survivors." Should be mindful about the language because the words you're using could force someone to be someone they are not.
Founder, Program manager
FORCE collective
"Centering the needs of survivors and creating the space and giving community tools to support survivors"
-What Monument Quilt stands for
"Survivors need community reconnection to reintegrate into society."
Interview takeaways:
  1. There is no end of recovery. It's learning to cope with what we've been through
  2. In a lot of campaigns, the actual survivors' experience gets lost and they don't always centers the needs of survivors.
  3. Community connection is an important part of healing from trauma.
  4. Survivors' experience are often out of social compact- meaning it's unimaginable for other people without the experience.
  5. Survivors need community reconnection to reintegrate into society.

Personas

Sam

She/her, 23, Waitress

Sam was raped by her uncle as a kid and didn't tell anyone until recently.

Sam's best friend is very supportive and wants to be there for her in her recovery.

Sam's family is very close to this uncle and Sam still has to be in the same space with him in family events once a few months but her family doesn't know about this attack.

Sam started going to a support group.

Dan

He/him, 20, College student

Dan was regularly touched by his classmate in his elementary school.

Dan's parents found about the abuse and took care about the situation with the school.

Dan had been going to counseling until he graduate from middle school.

Dan never talks about this experience with anybody because of the social stigma against male victims.

Jill

She/her, 17, High school student

Jill was raped by her middle school teacher.

Jill's parents are very supportive and want to help but Jill does not want to talk about this attack to anybody.

Jill's parents suggest she gets some help from supporting organizations but she refuses to bring up this attack to anybody.

Ellie

She/her, 32, Barista

Ellie is a transgender woman.

Ellie was raped by her partner which she is still in a relationship with. She was also raped by a stranger at a party whil she is in this relationship.

Ellie's partner got mad at her after Ellie told him about the attack at the party. Ellie's partner is not supportive of Ellie's recovery but often brings up this attack in their fight and blame Ellie for the attack.

Ellie doesn't talk to her parents at all.

Ellie is afraid to talk to her friends about her abusive relationship because she knows they will tell her to end the relationship.

Ellie goes to a support group but only talks about the attack at the party.

Melisa

She/her, 28, Program coordinator

Melisa is passionate about her work but always overloaded with work.

Melisa is a survivor of sexual violence herself. She understands her clients well.

She is open to trying new programs but she would appreciate a simple but effective program that she can facilitate.

Jannet

Jannet wants to support her daughter but the daughter doesn't want to talk about the experience or get any support.

Jannet is worried about her daughter because she sees difference in her daughter's behavior and school performance. Jannet is worried about her daughter's mental health but she doesn't know how to make her daughter get help without forcing her.

Betty

Betty's daughter recently told Betty that she was raped by her step dad which is Betty's current husband. Betty is confused if she should believe her daughter.

Prototype Design
Type of the prototype User interaction / Event / Publication (if applicable)
When + where? May 12 2018
Survivor Convening Event held by FORCE collective
Who? Participants of Survivor Convening Event
Survivors of sexual violence + possibly friends and family + support orgs workers
How?
  1. Table will be set up with a wall behind at the event
  2. On the table will be blank cards, pens, and color pencils
  3. Participants will be asked to write a letter(s) to their close friends and/or family about how they want to be supported. Below are some guide lines.
    • I want you to tell me ... when I'm feeling ...
    • I want you to go with me to ... because ...
    • I want to talk to you about ... I hope ...
  4. The cards will be collected and possibly published as books to be distributed by support organizations. If the participants want, the cards can be sent to whomever they want them to be received.
Hypothesis
  • By the act of writing a letter to close ones, the participants will be reminded that they are not alone and there are people around them who might willing to support them in their healing journey.
  • This experience will provide an opportunity to think about what kind of support individuals need.
  • When(if) the collection of writings gets published and distributed by support organizations, it will be educational for people without the experience of sexual violence and connecting moment for survivors.

How the experience was + What I would do differently
About the topic of sexual violence
  • Working on the topic around sexual violence as a survivor was personally very meaningful but on one side, retraumatizing. At some point in the process, I completely stopped working on the thesis, because I was too emotionally overwhelmed.
  • This topic is very personal and sensitive. I hope I was mindful and thoughtful enough to work on this topic and in the process did no harm.
  • Like one of my interviewees said, there is no end of the recovery for survivors of sexual violence. What we can do is to learn to cope with what we have been through. It is a slow process, and everybody's pace is different. I somehow feel rushed my recovery to work on this issue.
In the process
  • The level of intervention that I was looking at in the beginning of the project was the macrosystem. I think it was going to be better if I was more mindful about the time frame of this thesis project.
  • When asking questions to the public, questions should be easy to relate and simple. In my brainstorming sessions, I wish I asked questions that are less complicated but closer to individual's emotion and daily life.
  • Connecting with organizations is hard and it takes time. I would try to connect with organizations earlier in the whole process if I do it again.
